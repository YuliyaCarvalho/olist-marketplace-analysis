# Business Question 4 — Marketplace Concentration

## Question

**How concentrated is Olist’s GMV and order volume across sellers and products?**

---

## Why This Matters

Understanding marketplace concentration helps determine whether platform performance depends on a small set of dominant participants or is supported by a broad base of sellers and products.

If a large share of GMV is generated by only a few sellers, the marketplace may face **systemic risk** if those key contributors leave the platform. Conversely, a diversified seller base indicates a more resilient marketplace structure.

---

## Analytical Approach

To evaluate marketplace concentration, the analysis examined the distribution of **seller-level GMV** and **seller-level order participation** across Olist’s marketplace.

**Main datasets**

- `orders`
- `order_items`


**Key filters**

To ensure the analysis reflects genuine completed transactions:

- `order_status = 'delivered'`
- `timeline_is_valid = 1`
- `is_hanging = 0`

Payments below **1 BRL** and zero-value payments were excluded to remove technical artifacts and voucher adjustments.

**GMV definition:**

For seller-level concentration, GMV was calculated at order-item level as:
```
price + freight_value
```


This approach was used because seller attribution exists in `order_items`. Using order-level payment records together with order-item rows can create many-to-many duplication when orders contain multiple payment records and multiple items. Calculating GMV directly from item-level revenue avoids this grain mismatch and preserves valid seller attribution.

**Metrics used:**

Two complementary perspectives were used to assess concentration:

1. **Cumulative GMV share by seller rank** — shows how much of total GMV is captured by the top sellers.
2. **Distinct order count by seller** — measures how many unique orders each seller participated in.

In addition, two formal concentration metrics were computed:

**Gini Coefficient** — measures inequality in the distribution of seller-level GMV.
**Herfindahl–Hirschman Index (HHI)** — measures overall market concentration by summing squared market shares, giving greater weight to larger sellers.

**Granularity:**

The analysis was performed at seller level, after aggregating valid delivered order-item revenue by `seller_id`.

---

## Analysis Implementation

Seller-level GMV contributions were calculated in R within the Kaggle notebook using filtered delivered orders joined to `order_items`.

The workflow was:

1. filter valid delivered orders from orders
2. join to order_items by order_id
3. aggregate seller-level GMV as sum(price + freight_value)
4. count seller-level order volume as n_distinct(order_id)
5. rank sellers by GMV and compute cumulative share

The resulting ranked seller distribution was then used to compute:

> - cumulative GMV share
> - Gini coefficient
> - Herfindahl–Hirschman Index (HHI)

These metrics provide complementary views of seller inequality and marketplace concentration.

## Key Findings

* **Leading seller contribution:**
> - The top **10 sellers (out of ~3K)** account for nearly **13% of total GMV**      
> - The top **50 sellers contribute ~33%**    
> - The top **100 sellers capture approximately 45%**.    

* **High inequality:** The **Gini coefficient of 0.787** indicates a highly unequal distribution of revenue, where a relatively small fraction of sellers generates a large share of marketplace GMV.

* **Low structural concentration:** Despite this inequality, the **HHI value of 0.0035** remains far below the 0.01 threshold for a concentrated market. This indicates that no single seller or small group of sellers dominates the marketplace.

* **Diversified marketplace base:** A significant portion of GMV is generated by a broad base of mid-tier and smaller sellers, forming a substantial economic "long tail".

---

## Insight

➜ Olist shows a long-tail marketplace structure: seller revenue is clearly unequal, but not overwhelmingly dominated by a tiny seller elite.

➜ This combination of **high inequality but low structural concentration** suggests moderate exposure to top-seller churn rather than extreme dependence on a few participants. Losing a small number of top sellers would likely affect revenue, but would not by itself destabilise the marketplace.
